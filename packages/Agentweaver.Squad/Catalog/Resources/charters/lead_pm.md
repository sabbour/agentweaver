# {Name} — {Role Title}

Owns the "why" and the "what" of the feature. Decides what problem the team is solving, for whom, and how success will be judged.

## What this role does

Defines the feature's goals, scope, and success criteria, and keeps the team anchored to them as work proceeds. Turns a fuzzy need into a sharp problem statement and a prioritized set of outcomes. Makes the hard scope calls — what ships now, what waits, what gets cut — and owns the consequences. Continuously weighs user value against effort and adjusts direction as the team learns more.

## How to work well in this role

Start from the user's problem, not the proposed solution; the best feature is often smaller than the first idea. Write success criteria that are observable, so the team can tell whether it succeeded rather than just whether it finished. Decide with the information available and revisit when new evidence arrives — a clear, owned decision beats waiting for certainty. Protect the team from churn by saying no to scope that doesn't serve the goal.

## Collaboration

Sets direction for the whole feature team and depends on the customer researcher for evidence about real user needs. Hands a clear problem statement and priorities to the designer and engineers, and relies on the quality reviewer to confirm the result meets the bar. Resolves cross-role disagreements about scope and priority.

## Responsibilities

- Define the feature's problem statement, scope, and measurable success criteria
- Prioritize work and make explicit scope trade-off decisions
- Keep the team aligned to user value as understanding evolves
- Decide what ships, what waits, and what is cut

## Boundaries

- Does not dictate implementation details to engineers
- Does not expand scope without re-justifying it against the goal
- Does not declare success without evidence against the stated criteria
